First Aid

First Aid is the immediate help given to a sick or injured person before professional medical care arrives. The goal is to preserve life, prevent the condition from worsening , and promote recovery.


The “3 P’s” of First Aid:

  1. Preserve Life – Your top priority.

  2. Prevent Deterioration – Keep the person stable.

  3. Promote Recovery – Comfort, care, and reassurance.


Your First Aid Toolkit (Mental or Physical):

  • Stay calm. Panic helps no one.

  • Assess the situation : Are you safe? Is the person safe? Is there danger (electricity, gas, traffic)?

  • Use the ABC approach :

    • A – Airway : Make sure it’s clear (tilt head, check for blockage).

    • B – Breathing : Look, listen, feel for breath.

    • C – Circulation : Check for pulse and bleeding.


Immediate Do’s and Don'ts:

DO:

  • Reassure the person. Stay with them.

  • Keep them warm and comfortable .

  • Wear gloves or improvise with cloth to prevent infection (if bleeding is involved).

  • Follow the appropriate response for the condition (CPR, bleeding, fainting etc.).

  • Call for help early if there’s any doubt.

DON'T:

  • Don't move the person unless in danger (e.g., fire, traffic).

  • Don't give food or drink to someone unconscious, or in case of abdominal injuries.

  • Don’t play doctor — avoid administering unknown medications.


Golden Mental Checklist

(for quick first-aid triage)

  1. Safe scene?

  2. Are they conscious?

  3. Are they breathing?

  4. Bleeding?

  5. Call help?


Basic Physical First Aid Kit (good to keep in halls/rooms):

Most of these are available in campus as a kit.

  • Gauze, bandages, antiseptic wipes

  • Gloves (non-latex)

  • Small scissors & tweezers

  • Paracetamol, Cetirizine (basic meds)

  • ORS sachets

  • Allergy Meds

  • Emergency numbers list

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

CPR is an emergency life-saving procedure used when someone’s heart stops beating or they’ve stopped breathing. The goal is to maintain blood flow to the brain and organs until professional help arrives .

When to Use CPR

Start CPR if the person is:

  • Unconscious and unresponsive

  • Not breathing normally (gasping or no breath)

  • No pulse (if you're trained to check)


1. Ensure Safety First

Make sure the area is safe for both you and the victim (no fire, traffic, etc.).


2. Check Responsiveness

  • Tap the person and shout : “Are you okay?”

  • If no response, proceed.


3. Call for Help

  • Shout for help and assign someone to call emergency services (e.g., 108 in India).

  • If alone, call emergency first , put on speaker, and begin CPR.


4. Chest Compressions (Hands-Only CPR for untrained responders) - READ THIS

  • Kneel beside the chest .

  • Place heel of one hand in the center of the chest (between the nipples), other hand on top.

  • Lock elbows, use your upper body weight.

Compress at least 2 inches deep , at a rate of 100–120 compressions per minute
(Tip: Think of the rhythm of the song "Stayin’ Alive" by the Bee Gees)

  • Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions.

Continue until:

  • The person starts breathing or moving

  • Medical help arrives and takes over

  • You are too exhausted to continue


5. Rescue Breaths (Only if trained)

If you’re trained and confident:

  1. Give 30 compressions

  2. Then 2 breaths :

    • Tilt the head back slightly to open the airway.

    • Pinch the nose, cover their mouth with yours, and blow for 1 second .

    • Watch for chest rise.

Alternate 30 compressions + 2 breaths until help arrives.


6. Use an AED (Automated External Defibrillator).

I don't think so this is in campus, but it would be there in airports and such, so definitely useful.

  • Turn it on and follow voice instructions.

  • AED will guide whether to shock or continue compressions.

  • Resume CPR immediately after a shock , if advised.


Notes

  • Don’t worry about breaking ribs. It’s more important to keep blood flowing.

  • Don’t stop unless the person recovers, help arrives, or you're completely exhausted.

  • Always report the event to emergency services or staff.

B leeding (Cuts & Injuries)

Bleeding is one of the most common first aid situations. It can range from minor cuts to life-threatening injuries. The key is to act quickly and calmly to control the bleeding and prevent infection or shock .


Step-by-Step: What to Do

1. Stay Safe

  • Wash your hands or wear gloves if available.

  • If gloves aren't available, use a clean plastic bag, gauze, or cloth between your hand and the wound.

2. Apply Pressure

  • Use a clean cloth or gauze to press firmly on the wound.

  • If blood soaks through, don’t remove the cloth —add more layers and keep pressing.

  • Maintain pressure for several minutes (at least 5–10) without checking too often.

3. Raise the Wounded Part (if possible)

  • Elevate the injured area above heart level to slow bleeding (unless you suspect a fracture or spinal injury).

4. Clean the Wound (Minor Cuts Only)

  • For small cuts: rinse with clean water , then apply an antiseptic (like Dettol).

  • Don’t use hydrogen peroxide —it can damage tissue.

5. Bandage It

  • Cover with a sterile dressing or bandage .

  • For large wounds: wrap with gauze and secure it. Avoid wrapping too tight—you should be able to slide a finger underneath.


When to Seek Medical Help

Call for medical assistance or go to the hospital if:

  • Bleeding doesn’t stop after 10–15 minutes of pressure.

  • Blood is spurting (likely an artery).

  • There’s a deep or gaping wound , especially on the face or hands.

  • Foreign objects (glass, metal) are embedded— don’t remove them yourself.

  • Signs of infection appear later (pus, redness, swelling).

  • A tetanus booster is due (last shot >5 years ago).

  • There is a head injury or signs of shock (pale, dizzy, fainting, cold skin).

If you’re unsure whether stitches are needed, use this guideline:

“If you can see fat, muscle, or bone , or the wound won’t close easily or stop bleeding , get stitches.”


DON’T:

  • Don’t remove embedded objects.

  • Don’t use cotton directly on deep wounds.

  • Don’t panic at blood volume— focus on pressure and composure .


Internal Bleeding (Invisible but Dangerous)

  • Caused by blunt trauma (e.g., falls, crashes).

  • Watch for: dizziness, vomiting blood, swelling, bruising, fainting .

  • This is life-threatening. Call emergency services immediately.

Allergic Reactions

Allergic reactions occur when the immune system overreacts to a substance (allergen). While many are mild, some can escalate quickly into life-threatening anaphylaxis. Recognizing the signs early and responding swiftly is critical.


Common Triggers

  • Food (nuts, shellfish, eggs, dairy)

  • Insect stings (bees, wasps)

  • Medications (penicillin, aspirin)

  • Latex, dust, or pollen


Symptoms of a Mild Reaction

  • Hives or rash

  • Itchy or watery eyes

  • Sneezing, nasal congestion

  • Mild swelling (especially lips or face)

  • Nausea or stomach cramps

What to Do:

  1. Move away from the allergen if it’s airborne or environmental.

  2. Administer oral antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine, loratadine) if available and the person is conscious.

  3. Monitor symptoms. If they worsen, prepare for escalation.


Symptoms of a Severe Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

This is comparatively rare in India

  • Swelling of throat, tongue, or face

  • Difficulty breathing, wheezing

  • Rapid or weak pulse

  • Dizziness, confusion, or collapse

  • Nausea, vomiting

  • Pale or bluish skin

This is a medical emergency. Immediate action is critical.


What to Do in Case of Anaphylaxis

  1. Call an ambulance immediately. Time is critical: don’t wait to see if it improves.

  2. Use an epinephrine injector : if the person has one. Inject into the outer thigh (can be done through clothing). Unlikely

  3. Lie the person down and elevate their legs slightly unless they’re having trouble breathing (in which case, keep them upright).

  4. Do not give food or drink.

  5. If no improvement after 5–10 minutes, and a second epinephrine injector available, administer a second dose.

  6. Stay with them and keep them calm until medical help arrives.


Fainting (Syncope)

Fainting is a sudden, brief loss of consciousness caused by a temporary drop in blood flow to the brain. It usually resolves on its own within a minute or two, but it's important to treat it seriously—especially if it's unexplained .

Always report fainting episodes.


Common Causes

  • Standing too long or rising too quickly (postural hypotension)

  • Dehydration or hunger

  • Heat exhaustio n

  • Intense emotional stress or pain

  • Heart conditions (less common but serious)

  • Blood loss or injury


Warning Signs (Presyncope)

  • Light-headedness or dizziness

  • Nausea

  • Blurred or tunnel vision

  • Pale skin, sweating

  • Weak pulse or fast heartbeat

If these occur:

  1. Help the person sit or lie down immediately .

  2. Raise their legs slightly to improve blood flow to the brain.

  3. Loosen any tight clothing (especially around neck or chest).

  4. Offer water or juice once they’re stable and fully alert.

  5. Allow them to rest for at least 10–15 minutes .


If the Person Faints

  1. Lay them flat on their back . Ensure the area is safe.

  2. Check for responsiveness : Try calling their name, gently shaking them, or flicking water on their face.

  3. Check breathing and pulse . If absent, begin CPR and call for emergency help immediately.

  4. If breathing is normal , raise their legs and monitor them until they regain consciousness.

  5. Loosen tight clothing and keep the area cool and quiet.

  6. Do not give food or drink until they are fully conscious and alert.

  7. Once awake , help them sit up slowly to avoid a second faint.


When to Seek Medical Help

  • If the person doesn’t regain consciousness within 1–2 minutes

  • If fainting happened during exercise

  • If there’s chest pain, irregular heartbeat, or difficulty breathing

  • If they have a head injury from the fall

  • If fainting occurs repeatedly or without clear reason
