General Tips

Checklists

Unconscious

These steps should be followed in order to ensure the best possible response:

  1. Confirm they’re actually unconscious.
    Try calling out, gently shaking them, or sprinkling a bit of water on their face. If they don’t respond, assume they are unconscious and proceed.

  2. Check for breathing and pulse.
    Look for chest movement or feel for airflow near the nose. Simultaneously, check for a pulse at the neck or wrist.

    • If no breathing or no pulse, begin CPR immediately.

      • If you’re alone: start CPR.

      • If in a group: ask if anyone is trained and get them to take over if possible.

  3. Call an ambulance.

    • If alone: call yourself, even if in the middle of CPR (during a brief pause if needed).

    • If in a group: point at a specific person and clearly tell them to call. (Avoid shouting to a crowd : people tend to assume someone else will act.)

  4. Alert nearby staff.
    Ask someone specifically to get help from campus staff. This could be hall staff, faculty, security, etc. They can help coordinate the response or open access points for emergency services.

  5. Identify possible causes.
    Try to figure out what might have caused the unconsciousness: an injury, allergic reaction, heatstroke, or other issue. Prevent further harm if possible and report this information clearly to medical responders.

  6. Ensure proper handoff.
    Ideally, accompany the person to the medical center (usually BCRTH)m or at least make sure the attending staff know the situation in detail.

  7. Take care of yourself.
    Witnessing a medical emergency can be intense. Talk to someone you trust afterward. Please.

Conscious


If They Are Delirious or Confused (They aren’t thinking or responding properly)

  1. Treat it as a medical emergency.

This could be due to:

    • High fever (often from infection)

    • Head injury or trauma

    • A mental health episode (e.g., panic, psychosis, dissociation)

None of these should be ignored. Act immediately .

  1. Follow the unconscious person checklist – with adjustments:

    • Try to keep them calm and seated .

    • Ensure they are not left alone.

    • If the situation is not extreme (e.g., they can walk and speak somewhat), consider taking a Toto (camp auto) to BCRTH or the nearest medical facility.

    • If they are becoming aggressive, fainting, or worsening rapidly — call an ambulance.

If They Are Just Injured (and not Delirious)

  1. Stop the bleeding first.

    • Apply firm pressure using clean cloth, tissue, or sterile gauze.

    • For minor wounds:

      • Rinse gently with clean water.

      • Apply antiseptic (Dettol or similar).

      • Cover with a bandage.

    • For deeper cuts:

      • Use sterile gauze and cotton to cover the area.

      • Apply pressure and keep it elevated.

      • If bleeding doesn't stop in 10–15 minutes or soaks through gauze — seek medical help immediately . You likely need stitches .

  2. Get a tetanus shot (TT injection) if:

    • The wound was caused by metal, soil, or dirty surfaces.

    • You haven’t had a TT booster in the last 5–10 years, or a shot in the last year. If you're unsure, assume you  haven't had.
      ( Go to BCRTH or ask staff for direction. )

  3. Head Injury?

    • Even if there is no bleeding, do not ignore it.

    • Look for signs like drowsiness, confusion, vomiting, or blurred vision.

    • Go to a doctor or medical center ASAP.

Potentially Dangerous Situations

These are situations where no one may be visibly hurt yet , but there’s a serious risk of injury, medical emergency, or harm. These should not be taken lightly.


1. Fire, Smoke, or Gas Leak

  • Evacuate immediately – do not wait to verify the source.

  • If you smell gas (e.g., LPG leak), do NOT switch on/off any electrical devices or lights — it may trigger a spark.

  • Raise the alarm : inform security or staff ASAP.

  • Call for emergency help if needed. If indoors, keep doors open , and leave windows open if safe to do so.

  • Never try to be a hero — getting help fast is your first priority.


2. Electrical Hazards

  • If someone has been electrocuted , do NOT touch them directly — they might still be conducting electricity.

  • Turn off the power source (switch, circuit breaker) first.

  • Only then, move them using a non-conductive object (wooden stick, cloth).

  • If unconscious or unresponsive, follow the Unconscious checklist .


3. Aggressive or Violent Behavior (Including Mental Health Crises)

  • Keep a safe distance — do not try to restrain the person unless trained.

  • If you feel unsafe, leave the area and get help.

  • Alert campus security or nearby staff discreetly.

  • If you suspect a mental health crisis (e.g., hallucinating, shouting, threatening self-harm), call for help and stay nearby (if safe), but do not escalate the situation.


4. Severe Allergic Reaction Trigger (Food, Insect Sting, etc.)

If someone is starting to show signs of a severe allergic reaction (swelling of lips/face, difficulty breathing, rash/hives), act immediately :

  • Ask if they have an allergy medication and help them use it.

  • Call an ambulance immediately — anaphylaxis can become fatal within minutes .

  • If they lose consciousness, follow the Unconscious checklist .


5. Seizure or Collapse in Public.

If someone collapses or has a seizure:

  • Do not try to restrain them.

  • Clear the area of any sharp or hard objects.

  • Turn them on their side gently once the seizure ends.

  • Time the seizure if possible — if it lasts more than 5 minutes , or they don’t wake up afterward, call an ambulance.

Typical Ailments

These are the most common, everyday health issues — usually manageable, but still important to address early. Here's what to do and when to escalate.


1. Fevers

  • Mild to Moderate (below 39°C):

    • Rest and stay hydrated.

    • Take paracetamol (e.g., Dolo 650) as per dosage guidelines.

    • Avoid excessive blankets or heat — let the body cool naturally.

  • High Fever (above 102°F or persistent after 2 days):

    • Visit the medical center (BCRTH) — could be due to viral/bacterial infection.

    • Check for signs like confusion, rash, or difficulty breathing : these need urgent attention.

2. Cough, Cold, or Sore Throat

  • Use steam inhalation and salt-water gargles for throat relief.

  • Basic medication: paracetamol, antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine), or lozenges (if known to work for you).

  • Stay hydrated and avoid cold drinks.

  • If symptoms persist beyond 5 days, or worsen (e.g., wheezing, chest pain, or fever), visit a doctor.


3. Food Poisoning / Stomach Issues

  • If symptoms include vomiting, loose motion, or stomach pain :

    • Rest and take ORS (oral rehydration solution) to replace lost fluids.

    • Avoid dairy and oily food.

    • Take safe antacids or anti-spasmodic (like Digene, Cyclopam) only if you've used them before.

  • When to go to BCRTH:

    • Blood in stool or vomit, persistent vomiting (>2 times), or inability to keep fluids down.


4. Period Pain or Cramps

  • Use a hot water bag or heating pad for relief.

  • Over-the-counter medication like Meftal Spas can be taken if previously tolerated.

  • If pain is severe, irregular, or accompanied by dizziness or vomiting, consult a medical professional.


5. Mild Allergies (Itchy eyes, rashes, sneezing)

  • Identify and avoid the trigger if known (dust, food, etc.).

  • Take an antihistamine (e.g., cetirizine). It usually takes 30–60 mins to kick in.

  • If swelling occurs near lips or throat, or breathing is affected — treat as a medical emergency.


General Tips

  • Don't self-medicate blindly. Stick to medicines you’ve taken before or have been prescribed.

  • When in doubt, visit BCRTH or ask staff. Better early than too late.

  • Drink water. Seriously, it's boring advice but solves half of all student health issues.
